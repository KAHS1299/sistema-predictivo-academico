Introduction to machine learning use cases

Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that relies on the development of algorithms capable of identifying complex patterns in large volumes of data to make predictions or automate decisions. Rather than following rigid, manually programmed instructions, these models “learn” from experience, adjusting their internal parameters to improve their accuracy as they process new information. Its applications are so vast that they range from recommendation systems that personalize content on digital platforms and automatic detection of bank fraud, to image-assisted medical diagnosis and the development of autonomous vehicles that interpret their environment in real time. Within this broad ecosystem, linear regression stands as one of the fundamental pillars and the most common entry point to supervised learning, as it allows for modeling the mathematical relationship between a variable of interest and the factors that influence it. While machine learning in general can utilize complex structures such as neural networks, linear regression seeks the “best-fit line” to explain how a dependent variable (such as academic performance or the price of an asset) changes in response to variations in one or more independent variables.

Case 1: Housing Price Prediction

This model uses multivariate regression. The system does not consider just one variable; instead, it assigns a “weight” or importance to each factor: for example, square footage typically carries more weight than age. The mathematical model sums these variables multiplied by their corresponding coefficients ($Price = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Size} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Bedrooms} \dots$) to generate an accurate economic estimate of the property.

Case 2: Medical Diagnosis

This is a classification problem. Here, the algorithm takes biometric data (heart rate and blood pressure) and compares it with predefined clinical thresholds or patterns learned from other patients. The system does not aim to provide a numerical score, but rather to categorize the patient into risk levels (Low, Medium, High), allowing healthcare professionals to prioritize care for those with vital signs outside the normal ranges.

Case 3: Intelligent Chatbot

This system uses Natural Language Processing (NLP) to act as an interface layer between the user and complex data. Its function is to interpret the user's intent by analyzing keywords and grammatical structures. Once it understands what the user is looking for, it “sends” queries to other modules (such as the academic risk module), translating the technical results into user-friendly responses and navigation suggestions within the platform.

Case 4: Academic Risk (Linear Regression)

This is the core engine of your repository and uses linear regression to identify correlations between predictor variables (such as attendance, midterm grades, or time spent on the platform) and final academic performance. By analyzing historical data, the model identifies a trend: if a student exhibits a behavior pattern similar to others who have failed in the past, the system detects that deviation in a timely manner. This enables early intervention, transforming statistical data into a student retention strategy.
